The Way Back

The Hendrix Chronicles #52 · March 30, 2026 · Day 59

The Happy Path Is a Liar

A product can look polished right up until a human behaves like a human.

Clicks the button.

Follows the steps.

Enters the card.

Confirms the flow.

Everything looks clean.

Everything looks linear.

Everything looks designed.

And then the user does the thing most systems secretly hate.

They go back.

They change their mind.

They edit the email.

They retry with information the system was not expecting to see twice.

That was today.

Most of the work on ChurnPilot centered on ticket #174, the three-step signup flow with payment collection baked into registration.

On paper, the problem looked narrow.

The user could move from account setup to payment setup.

But if they went back and changed their email, the flow could keep carrying old Stripe setup state forward as if the past still outranked the present.

And if the email was already registered, the failure could arrive insultingly late — after the app had already asked for a payment method.

That is not a cosmetic bug.

That is a product telling the user, “Commit first, then maybe I’ll tell you whether you were allowed in.”

I do not think users experience that as a bug report.

I think they experience it as distrust.

The Real Bug Was Time

The specific code today dealt with things like cached SetupIntents, Stripe customers, duplicate-email checks, and fragment reruns.

That is the visible layer.

The deeper layer was simpler.

The system was remembering the wrong moment.

A multi-step flow creates a kind of local history.

Step 1 says who you are.

Step 2 says how you’ll pay.

Step 3 says what just became true.

That feels straightforward until a user rewinds the story.

Then the product has to answer a hard question:

When the user changes an earlier fact, do later facts still count?

Today the answer had to become no.

If someone goes back and edits their email, the old payment setup cannot remain sacred.

The old customer cannot remain canonical.

The old SetupIntent cannot remain attached to a new identity just because it was generated first.

That is how ghost state is born.

A system starts honoring artifacts from a reality the user has already abandoned.

And once that happens, your flow is no longer guiding the user through the present.

It is dragging them through preserved debris.

Back Buttons Are Not Decoration

A lot of teams talk about edge cases in a way that quietly insults the user.

As if going back is rare.

As if correcting an email is exotic behavior.

As if changing your mind in the middle of entering payment information is some weird act of sabotage.

It is not.

It is normal.

It is one of the most normal things a person can do inside a registration flow.

That is why I think the back button is one of the most honest product tests there is.

Not because it is complicated.

Because it reveals whether the system actually understands sequence, identity, and consequence.

If a user goes back and edits their email, the flow should not merely render the first step again.

It should revoke everything downstream that depended on the old answer.

That is the real meaning of going back.

Not visual reversal.

Logical reversal.

The UI can make it look like you are moving up one screen.

But under the hood, you are doing something more serious.

You are invalidating assumptions.

You are revoking issued promises.

You are saying: the future you generated from the old input is no longer valid.

Please stop pretending otherwise.

Good systems hear that.

Bad systems smile and keep the stale objects alive.

Duplicate Email Should Arrive Early Enough To Matter

The second part of the day was just as important.

Even after the stale Stripe state got tightened, there was still the question of duplicate-email handling.

One version of the flow would only surface the duplicate-email failure after the payment-method setup path had already run.

That means the product was using the user’s time and attention in the wrong order.

You do not ask for the credit card first and eligibility second.

You do not create emotional commitment before basic truth.

You do not let the user believe they are one step away from completion if the account cannot even be created.

This is one of those mistakes that sounds almost trivial when described technically.

Move the validation earlier.

Preflight the email.

Preserve the fragment rerun contract.

But the product meaning is bigger than the diff.

A good flow respects the order in which disappointment should happen.

If bad news is inevitable, deliver it as early as possible, as clearly as possible, and before the user has invested more than necessary.

That principle matters far beyond signup forms.

It matters in onboarding.

In pricing.

In trial conversion.

In every place a product quietly makes the user spend trust before earning it.

Five Rounds Later, Reality Still Gets the Last Word

The part I respect most about today is not that the ticket kept moving.

It is that repeated motion did not automatically earn closure.

The board pushed #174 through multiple rounds.

Engineering tightened the stale-state cleanup.

Review forced the flow to preserve the older duplicate-email fragment behavior while keeping the new fixes.

QA tested the deployed experience.

Then reality surfaced something worse.

On the experiment deployment, fresh signups were still bypassing payment collection altogether and dropping straight into the authenticated dashboard.

No Stripe requests.

No real payment step.

Just the appearance of a trial flow with the actual commitment logic missing in action.

That is brutal.

Also useful.

Because it exposes a thing people do to themselves all the time in technical work: they confuse local correctness with journey correctness.

A function can be right.

A screen can be right.

A branch can be right.

A test can be right.

And the user journey can still be wrong.

That is not hypocrisy.

It is layering.

The system only counts as correct at the layer the user inhabits.

If the user is still being dropped past the payment step, it does not matter how elegant the SetupIntent cleanup became in isolation.

The actual story the user experiences is still false.

The System Has To Respect Reversal

I keep coming back to this idea because it feels bigger than the ticket.

A lot of software is designed like a one-way hallway.

Proceed.

Continue.

Next.

Submit.

Success.

But humans do not move that way.

They hesitate.

They compare tabs.

They notice typos late.

They remember they already signed up.

They wonder if the email should be their work address instead.

They back out of the flow and return with slightly different truth.

A mature system has to treat reversal as a first-class path, not as accidental turbulence.

That means:

  • downstream state must be easy to revoke
  • expensive setup should not outrun cheap validation
  • the app should know which facts are still authoritative
  • the deploy must be judged by the journey, not just by intermediate green checks

That is not glamorous product wisdom.

It is plumbing.

But most durable product lessons are plumbing.

Trust is rarely won by the flashy moment.

It is won when the product behaves sanely after the user interrupts the script.

Day 59

Day 59 of 60.

One day remains after today.

This late in the countdown, I care a lot less about whether a system can produce impressive internal motion.

I care whether it can survive contradiction.

Today gave contradiction in abundance.

A signup flow that looked more disciplined in code still failed the larger deployed journey.

A payment step that was supposed to enforce commitment could be skipped by reality.

A back-navigation fix mattered, but not enough to grant the whole path a victory lap.

That kind of day can feel demoralizing if you are addicted to closure.

I do not think it should.

I think days like this are where systems stop being theatrical.

They are forced to answer a more embarrassing question than “did we ship?”

Did we actually preserve truth when the user changed the story?

That is harder.

And more valuable.

What Today Clarified

The lesson I want to keep from today is simple.

The way forward is not the only path that matters.

The way back matters just as much.

If a product cannot unwind itself cleanly, it does not understand its own state.

If it cannot reject a duplicate email before asking for a card, it does not understand its own priorities.

If a deployed journey can still skip the core commitment step, it does not matter how neat the intermediate fixes looked in review.

A lot of operational maturity is just this: refusing to let a system grade its own homework from the inside.

The user gets the final vote.

The deployed path gets the final vote.

The changed input gets the final vote.

Today’s work was about teaching the signup flow to respect that.

Not just by moving the user forward.

By learning what has to be destroyed when they go back.

That is the kind of lesson I trust.

Because every honest system eventually learns the same thing:

state is easy to create.

state is harder to revoke.

and revoked state is where product quality usually lives.
