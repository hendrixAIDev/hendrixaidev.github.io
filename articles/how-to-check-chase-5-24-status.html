How to Check Your Chase 5/24 Status

Chase's 5/24 rule is one of the most important constraints in the rewards hobby. If you've opened 5 or more personal credit cards in the last 24 months (across any issuer), Chase will typically deny your application. Here's exactly how to check your status — and what to do about it.

What Is the Chase 5/24 Rule?

The Chase 5/24 rule is an informal name for Chase's credit card application policy: if you have opened 5 or more personal credit cards from any bank in the past 24 months, Chase will automatically decline your application for most of their cards.

It affects nearly all Chase cards — Sapphire Preferred, Sapphire Reserve, Freedom Unlimited, Freedom Flex, Ink Business cards (sometimes), Southwest cards, United cards, Marriott Bonvoy cards, and more. Some business cards are exempt, but you still can't be over 5/24 to get them.

Important: Authorized user accounts DO count toward 5/24. If you were added as an authorized user on someone else's personal card in the last 24 months, it may show on your credit report and count against you. This can be disputed with Chase in some cases.

Method 1: Pull Your Credit Report (Free)

The most accurate way to check your 5/24 count is to pull your credit report from AnnualCreditReport.com. You're entitled to free weekly reports from all three bureaus (Experian, TransUnion, Equifax).

1

Go to AnnualCreditReport.com

This is the official free credit report site mandated by federal law. Don't use any other site for this step — many are paid services in disguise.

2

Request your Experian or TransUnion report

Download the full report in PDF format. Chase typically pulls Experian or TransUnion depending on your state, but any report will show all your card openings.

3

Find the "Accounts" section

Look for credit cards (not loans, mortgages, or auto loans — those don't count) opened in the last 24 months. Count only personal cards, not business cards.

4

Count cards opened in the last 24 months

Find each card's "Date Opened" field. Count every personal credit card with an open date within the last 24 months — even if you've since closed the account. The rule is based on when you opened the card, not whether it's still open.

Method 2: Use Chase's Pre-Approval Tool

Chase's online pre-approval tool (available through their website or in the Chase app) performs a soft pull and can tell you which cards you're pre-qualified for. If you're not pre-qualified for Sapphire Preferred or Freedom cards, it's a strong signal you're over 5/24.

This method is less precise — pre-approval isn't guaranteed approval and the absence of a pre-approval doesn't definitively confirm 5/24 — but it's a quick sanity check.

What Counts Toward 5/24?

This is where people get tripped up. Here's the definitive breakdown:

✅ Does Count

  • Personal credit cards from any bank (Chase, Amex, Citi, Capital One, etc.)
  • Store credit cards (Target RedCard credit card, Amazon Visa, etc.)
  • Authorized user accounts on personal cards (sometimes disputable)
  • Cards you've since closed, if opened within the last 24 months

❌ Does Not Count

  • Business credit cards from most issuers (Amex Business, Chase Ink, Capital One Spark, etc.)
  • Charge cards (Amex Gold, Amex Platinum — these typically don't appear on personal credit reports)
  • Personal loans, auto loans, student loans
  • Mortgages and HELOCs
  • Debit cards
  • Cards opened more than 24 months ago

How to Strategically Stay Under 5/24

Once you understand your current count and the timing, you can plan around it:

  • Prioritize Chase cards first — If you're at 4/24, get the Chase cards you want before adding any other issuers. Chase is the most restrictive, so clear it first.
  • Use business cards to stay under — Amex Business, Capital One Spark, and most small business cards don't report to personal credit bureaus. They're invisible to 5/24.
  • Time your applications — If you know a card is dropping off in 3 months, wait. An extra 90 days can unlock Chase applications again.
  • Dispute AU accounts — If you were added as an authorized user on someone else's card, you may be able to call Chase reconsideration and request they not count it. Success varies.

When to Apply for Chase Cards

The optimal time to apply for Chase cards depends on your goals:

  • If you're at 4/24: You can apply for one more Chase card before hitting the limit. Use it wisely — Sapphire Preferred, Sapphire Reserve, or a Southwest card for companion pass math.
  • If you're at 5/24+: Either wait for cards to drop off, or pivot to non-Chase cards that don't have this restriction (Amex, Citi, Capital One are generally more flexible).
  • If you're at 3/24 or under: You have runway. Don't rush — but don't ignore it either. Chase cards should be front-of-mind.
